Subspecialty map

Eight windows into modern neurosurgery.

Neurosurgery is usually divided into focused service lines because each group of disorders demands different anatomy knowledge, operative strategies, and long-term care models. The categories below are a concise synthesis of common neurosurgery subspecialty groupings used by academic departments and professional sections.

How to read this page

Each card pairs a short explanatory blurb with a custom illustration. The goal is not to be exhaustive, but to give a clear visual sense of how the field branches into vascular, tumor, spine, pediatric, functional, skull base, peripheral nerve, and cerebrospinal fluid focused practice.

Cerebrovascular

Cerebrovascular and endovascular surgery

Focuses on disorders of blood vessels in the brain and spinal cord, including aneurysms, arteriovenous malformations, cavernous malformations, and some stroke-related emergencies. Treatment may involve open microsurgery, catheter-based endovascular therapy, or both.

Spine

Spine surgery

Covers degenerative disease, disc herniation, instability, deformity, fractures, spinal tumors, and spinal cord compression. Surgeons in this area often blend decompression, reconstruction, instrumentation, and minimally invasive techniques.

Tumor care

Neuro-oncology

Centers on primary and metastatic tumors of the brain, spine, meninges, and related structures. The work often involves maximizing safe resection while coordinating closely with radiation oncology, medical oncology, neuropathology, and rehabilitation.

Skull base

Skull base surgery

Deals with lesions tucked around the underside of the brain, cranial nerves, major arteries, and the upper cervical junction. These operations often combine microsurgical and endoscopic routes because the anatomy is deep, narrow, and densely packed.

Pediatric

Pediatric neurosurgery

Focuses on neurosurgical problems in infants, children, and adolescents, such as congenital malformations, hydrocephalus, epilepsy, tumors, trauma, and spasticity. Pediatric care is distinct because the nervous system, skull, spine, and family context are all still developing.

Functional

Functional and epilepsy surgery

Treats disorders of brain networks rather than visible mass lesions alone, including epilepsy, Parkinson disease, tremor, dystonia, pain, and some psychiatric conditions. Techniques may involve stereotactic targeting, ablation, neuromodulation, and implanted stimulation systems.

Peripheral nerve

Peripheral nerve surgery

Concentrates on nerves outside the brain and spinal cord, including compression syndromes, traumatic injuries, tumors, nerve transfers, and brachial plexus reconstruction. The aim is often to recover function, reduce pain, or restore signal flow to muscles and skin.

CSF pathways

Hydrocephalus, CSF disorders, and neuroendoscopy

Covers problems related to cerebrospinal fluid circulation, ventricular anatomy, shunts, endoscopic third ventriculostomy, cyst fenestration, and related obstructive conditions. This area often blends precise navigation, imaging, and long-term device management.

Why these eight?

They capture the major ways departments commonly organize clinical expertise while staying readable for a first-pass overview. In real practice, there is overlap, and many neurosurgeons work across more than one adjacent area.
